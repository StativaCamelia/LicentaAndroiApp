<span style="color:#ba6b6c;"><b>Speech disorders</b></span> can affect the way a person creates sounds to form words. Certain voice disorders may also be considered speech disorders.

One of the most commonly experienced speech disorders is stuttering. Other speech disorders include apraxia and dysarthria.
<ul>
<li><p><span style="color:#ba6b6c;"><b>Apraxia</b></span> is a motor speech disorder caused by damage to the parts of the brain related to speaking.</p></li>
<li><p><span style="color:#ba6b6c;"><b>Dysarthria</b></span> is a motor speech disorder in which the muscles of the mouth, face, or respiratory system may become weak or have difficulty moving.</p></li>
</ul>
Some people with speech disorders are aware of what they would like to say but unable to articulate their thoughts. This may lead to self-esteem issues and the development of depression.

Speech disorders can affect adults and children. Early treatment can correct these conditions.
<h2 style="color:#ba6b6c;">What causes speech disorders?</h2>

<span style="color:#ba6b6c;"><b>Speech disorders affect the vocal cords, muscles, nerves, and other structures within the throat.</b></span>

Causes may include:
<ul>
<li>vocal cord damage</li>
<li>brain damage</li>
<li>muscle weakness</li>
<li>respiratory weakness</li>
<li>strokes</li>
<li>polyps or nodules on the vocal cords</li>
<li>vocal cord paralysis</li>
</ul>
People who have certain medical or developmental conditions may also have speech disorders.<span style="color:#ba6b6c;"><b> Common conditions that can lead to speech disorders are</b></span>:

<ul>
<li>autism</li>
<li>attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD)</li>
<li>strokes</li>
<li>oral cancer</li>
<li>laryngeal cancer</li>
<li>Huntington’s disease</li>
<li>dementia</li>
<li>amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS), also known as Lou Gehrig’s disease</li>
</ul>
Speech disorders may be hereditary, and they can develop over time.
